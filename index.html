About the Workshop

Forecasting has long been a central problem in machine learning, with mature traditions in time-series analysis, online learning, and conformal prediction. Yet recent advances in foundation models raise a qualitatively new question: can general-purpose AI systems reliably anticipate future events across diverse real-world domains?

We argue that this ability—forecasting in open domains under uncertainty—constitutes a new frontier of machine intelligence. This workshop proposes to study forecasting not merely as a predictive task, but as a cognitive capability we term mechanical prospection: the ability of an artificial system to systematically construct, evaluate, and update probabilistic beliefs over future events using algorithmic reasoning and real-world feedback.

Mechanical prospection requires calibrated uncertainty estimation, causal and temporal reasoning, critical assessment of information credibility, and evaluation grounded in realized outcomes rather than retrospective benchmarks.

The workshop aims to bring together researchers from machine learning, statistics, economics, and decision sciences to establish forecasting as a first-class object of study in ML. Through invited talks, contributed posters, and panel discussions, we will explore new methodologies, benchmarks, and evaluation paradigms for forecasting-capable AI systems.

Social Impact

Reliable forecasting under uncertainty is central to many high-stakes societal domains, including finance, public policy, public health, climate science, and national security. Improved AI forecasting systems could support better decision-making, enhance market efficiency, and improve the allocation of scarce resources.

However, overconfident or poorly calibrated forecasts may amplify misinformation or introduce systemic risks. By emphasizing uncertainty, calibration, and outcome-grounded evaluation, this workshop promotes responsible development and deployment of forecasting-capable AI systems.

Call for Papers

We invite submissions on all aspects of AI forecasting, from methodological advances to benchmark design to applications in real-world domains. Papers should be submitted via OpenReview and will undergo peer review by our program committee.

Accepted papers will be presented as posters during the workshop, with selected papers invited for oral presentations. A best paper award will be given at the closing session.

Topics of Interest

The workshop will cover (but is not limited to) the following topics:

  • Forecasting via generative/foundation models and agentic design (e.g., forecasting agents, LLM-as-a-Prophet)
  • Evaluation protocols and metrics for forecasting (e.g., forecasting benchmarks, automated forecasting event generation)
  • Reasoning paradigms for forecasting tasks (e.g., probabilistic reasoning, calibration, uncertainty quantification, causal inference)
  • Information retrieval and source search for forecasting (e.g., agentic search architecture, credibility assessment, evidence aggregation)
  • The incentives and economics of forecasting (e.g., prediction markets, calibration)
  • The learning and decision theory of forecasting (e.g., scoring rules, online learning)
  • Social, ethical, and governance issues in AI-driven forecasting

Submission Guidelines

Format: Submissions should be up to 4 pages (excluding references and appendix) using the ICLR 2026 template.

Anonymity: All submissions should be anonymized for double-blind review.

Dual Submission: We welcome submissions of work that has been previously published or is under review elsewhere, with proper disclosure.

Platform: Submissions will be handled through OpenReview.
